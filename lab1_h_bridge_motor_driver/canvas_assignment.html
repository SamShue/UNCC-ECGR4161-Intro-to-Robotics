Introduction

In this lab you will learn how an H-bridge circuit controls the direction and braking behavior of a DC motor. You will write Python code that maps high-level motor commands ("forward", "reverse", "stop", "brake") to the correct combination of four transistor switch states, then verify your logic against an animated circuit simulation.

Understanding H-bridge control is fundamental to robotics: virtually every mobile platform that uses DC motors relies on this circuit topology to achieve bidirectional motion.

Background

An H-bridge uses four switches — Q1, Q2, Q3, and Q4 — arranged so that the motor sits between two switch legs. By closing different pairs of switches you route current through the motor in either direction, or you short both motor terminals to ground to brake it.

+VCC
 |         |
[Q1]     [Q2]
 |         |
 +----M----+
 |         |
[Q3]     [Q4]
 |         |
GND

The four operating modes and their required switch states are:

CommandQ1Q2Q3Q4Result
"forward"ONOFFOFFONCurrent flows A → B (motor spins forward)
"reverse"OFFONONOFFCurrent flows B → A (motor spins in reverse)
"stop"OFFOFFOFFOFFMotor terminals floating — coasts to a stop
"brake"OFFOFFONONBoth terminals tied to GND — motor brakes

⚠ Never turn on Q1 and Q3 (or Q2 and Q4) simultaneously. This creates a shoot-through condition — a direct short from VCC to GND that can permanently damage the circuit.
